We want to engage the student in the solving of their own problem for a few reasons. One, it helps them take ownership of their work, which invests them in doing well in their class. Two, it creates and improves problem solving techniques that they can apply to a variety of subjects in school. Lastly, it allows the student to ratain more information so in the future they will be able to solve similar problems on their own.
Telling the student what to do may solve the problem, but it usually fails to meet the three goals mentioned earlier. In those illustrative sessions, you simply tell the student the answer. Instead, we can ask leading questions, for instance: "are you familiar with the Present Tense?", "How do you think we can conjugate the verb 'querer' in this sentence?" or "What would be your first step?". In this way we create a situation where the student has to think about what they have learned about this concept and tell us an action plan to move forward with the problem. The following example sessions may be helpful to give you some ideas: https://www.tutor.com/providers/content_templates/engagementexamplesfinal1

Policies and Procedures
I have also noticed that some sessions ended abruptly, for example: 09/04/2024 08:08 PM and 09/24/2024 08:08 PM, while some others were transferred due to being "out of scope": 07/25/2024 08:57 PM and 08/18/2024 04:42 PM. If a request appears to be out of scope for the subject, please accept and redirect the student so they are not left waiting in a queue where they cannot get help. Do not transfer these sessions, as the student will get sent to another tutor in the same incorrect subject. In this session 08/18/2024 04:42 PM the student waited for 1 hour after you transferred her to get another tutor who told her to reconnect under the correct subject. You can reference the Basic Concepts List to determine what is in scope, and see the Tutor Resource Manual for more on routing out of scope requests.

In this session 09/08/2024 04:59 PM the student was unresponsive for 15 minutes. This does occur from time to time due to various circumstances, and we have a procedure in place for how to handle these situations. As the Tutor Resource Manual states, when a student is unresponsive (either right away or becomes unresponsive during the session), a tutor should wait no more than a total of 4-8 minutes for the student to respond. Using the prescripts available (for example: "I'll only be able to wait a few more minutes...","Since I haven't heard from you..."), we can give the student opportunities to respond, and when no response is given, even after sending the prescripts, we can end the session.

Artificial Intelligence
As a tutor, it’s important to understand how to use this tool, to support students while maintaining integrity in the learning process.
I noticed that you referred to ChatGPT in some of your sessions. For example: 07/21/2024 06:08 PM :v
Joan V (Tutor)0:04:38 so I asked gpt and this is what it had to say
Joan V (Tutor)0:06:19 gpt says it is passive
Joan V (Tutor)0:10:42 gpt says its a pronoun

AI can be utilized to research information or to verify an answer on the tutor’s behalf to help guide a session. However, in no circumstance should AI generated feedback be copied and pasted into the chat taking the place of meaningful discussion between tutor and student. We should not be using AI to create responses to students. I encourage you to review the expectations as noted in the Basic Concepts List (http://www.tutor.com/cmspublicfiles/WWW/concept-list.pdf) and brush up on topics with which you may be uncertain. Additionally, tutors should never input student’s work into AI. Even though our intentions might be good, we must adhere to our personal information and privacy policies and avoid running student’s work through outside engines.

Conclusion
While you are doing a good job helping some students, we need to see improvement in your approach. Let's work on engaging students, working together, guiding them to find their own answers and solve their problems on their own.
